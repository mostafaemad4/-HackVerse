Risks you face while learning..

When entering the field of hacking (either ethically or for penetration testing), you will face a set of risks and challenges that you should be aware of to avoid potential consequences. Here are the most important risks: Hacking without permission: Any attempt to hack or test tools on systems or sites without permission is considered illegal. This can lead to: Hefty financial penalties. And imprisonment in some countries. Even “simple experiments” on public sites or devices can be considered a crime But if you want to start you have to know:

1. Hacking without permission (illegal)

Hacking without permission is the act of entering or attempting to access systems, networks, or digital data without obtaining official permission from the owner. This practice is considered illegal and dangerous, whether it is done for the purpose of exploration, curiosity, or for malicious purposes such as unauthorized penetration testing: testing tools and codes on sites or networks that you do not have the right to

2. Downloading tools and files from untrusted sources

Downloading tools from untrusted websites can expose you to significant risks that threaten your device and personal data. Here’s what could happen: Your Device Gets Infected with Malware and Viruses: Destroy files and damage your system. Ransomware: Encrypt your files and demand payment to unlock them. Some tools may contain Backdoors, which are hidden software that allows hackers to access and control your system without your knowledge.

3. Relying on ready-made versions of attacks

Relying on pre-made attacks means using tools or scripts created by others to perform certain attacks without understanding how they work. While it may seem like a quick and easy approach, it carries many risks and drawbacks. 4. Neglecting to learn the basics of the field

Neglecting the fundamentals of hacking can significantly affect your progress and effectiveness as an ethical hacker or cybersecurity professional. Here’s what could happen: Jumping straight into advanced tools and attacks without understanding the basics leaves you with shallow knowledge.

5. Working without protection and security for yourself

Working in hacking or cybersecurity without protection tools exposes you to significant risks, whether you’re testing systems, learning, or performing ethical hacking tasks. Here’s what could happen: Your device could be infected with viruses, Trojans, or keyloggers.

Definition

Black Hacker

A Black Hat Hacker is someone who uses their technical skills to hack systems and networks for malicious or illegal purposes. These hackers exploit vulnerabilities without permission to achieve personal or financial gain or to harm others.

White Hacker

A White Hat Hacker is an ethical hacker who uses their skills to help organizations identify and fix security vulnerabilities. They work with permission from the system owner to perform security assessments, conduct penetration tests, and provide advice on improving cybersecurity. White Hat Hackers play a key role in protecting systems and networks from malicious attacks.

Green Hacker

A Green Hat Hacker is a beginner or relatively inexperienced individual in the field of cybersecurity. They usually have a strong desire to learn hacking techniques and programming but may not yet possess the skills or experience to be a professional hacker. A Green Hat Hacker is still in the learning phase, often testing new tools and techniques, but they might make mistakes due to a lack of experience.

Positive role of hacking

Hackers play a positive role in our lives and in companies ethically, as they protect systems from attacks and discover security vulnerabilities in companies' systems before they are exploited by malicious hackers. They also help companies strengthen their security systems to ensure sensitive data is protected from theft or destruction.
